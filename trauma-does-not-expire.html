Why Trauma Doesn't Expire

The myth of passive recovery — and what the science says is waiting for us if we wait.

This page discusses the long-term biological and psychological effects of unresolved trauma, including content that may be difficult for survivors of serious or prolonged abuse. If you're in a fragile place right now, it's okay to come back to this when you're ready. If you're in crisis, support is here.

18 min read
// The Myth

You've Heard the Lines.
They're Bullshit.

You've heard the lines. Time heals all wounds. You just need distance. Focus on the future. Maybe you've said them to yourself. Maybe you believed them long enough to build a life around them.

They're bullshit.

Not well-intentioned-but-incomplete bullshit. Not mostly-true-with-some-exceptions bullshit. Functionally, dangerously wrong — because the belief that unresolved trauma resolves on its own if you just keep moving is exactly the belief that ensures it doesn't. Some people get lucky — their trauma gets intercepted early, sometimes deliberately, sometimes by accident, and they find their way through. This page isn't about them. You don't passive-recover from a broken leg you never set. The bone heals — just not straight. And the longer you wait, the more the body builds itself around the damage.

I spent years operating on exactly this logic. If I wasn't actively thinking about it, it couldn't still be affecting me — right? If I'd put enough distance between myself and what happened, surely that counted for something. It did count for something. It just wasn't healing. It was management. And management, it turns out, has a shelf life.

This page is for anyone still wrestling with that same logic. The person who got sober and figured that was the hard part. The person who has put years — maybe decades — between themselves and what happened, and wonders if that distance is doing the work. The person who is functional, maybe even successful, and isn't sure what there is left to address. If any part of that sounds familiar, the science on this page is worth sitting with.

Here's what it says.

// Henry
What Survives Everything Else

Dementia had stripped away every layer of cognition and left the emotional core standing.

In 2014, a documentary called Alive Inside introduced the world to a man named Henry. At the age of 94, Henry was in a late stage of dementia — largely unresponsive, slumped in his wheelchair, absent from the room in most of the ways we use to measure presence. Then someone put headphones on him and played the music of his youth.

He came back.

Not permanently. Not fully. But the footage is unmistakable — Henry singing, moving, there in a way he hadn't been. And for a short time after the music stopped, he could hold a conversation. The emotional and autobiographical memory systems that dementia hadn't yet reached were briefly, powerfully online.

I watched that documentary for the first time about 10 years ago. I was younger then, and Henry was old, and there was a comfortable distance between us that let me watch it as something moving but essentially remote. Except it wasn't remote. Because I watched him light up with joy and I couldn't stop the question that followed:

If that's what joy does — if positive emotion survives that intact — what about the other end of the spectrum?

I was not, at that point, far into any meaningful recovery. What I knew with certainty was that my most painful memories had not faded with time. They had not softened. If anything, certain ones had intensified — arriving with the same force, sometimes more, as the day they were made. I could not rationally convince myself that by the time I was Henry's age, they would simply be gone. Nothing in my experience suggested that was how this worked.

I'm 40 now. By most reckonings, roughly halfway. Henry is no longer a distant old man I watched on a screen — he's a mirror. And watching him come alive with something as simple as a song, I found myself less focused on Henry and more focused on a quieter question:

If I make it to 94 without addressing what I've been carrying — what gets to the surface when everything else is gone?

// A Note on Henry Specifically

I'm not drawing a line from trauma to dementia. That's not the argument here. Henry matters because dementia did something that nothing else can — it stripped away every layer of cognition and left the emotional core standing. What the Alive Inside footage shows us is what was underneath all along. Music reached it. Joy reached it. And if you've spent any time carrying trauma you haven't addressed, you already know what else reaches it. The mechanism doesn't care whether the trigger is a song you loved at twenty or something considerably darker. The depth of the storage is the point.

The Brain Has Been Covering for You
The prefrontal cortex has been running interference on your amygdala for decades. That's not healing. It's management — and it has a shelf life.
The Science
The Brain Stops Compensating

Here's what most people don't account for when they decide to wait trauma out: the brain has been working overtime to help you function.

The prefrontal cortex — the part responsible for regulation, context, rational override — has been running interference on your amygdala. Every time a threat signal fires that doesn't match the current reality, the prefrontal cortex steps in. Not now. Not here. Keep moving. It's not healing. It's management. And it keeps working as long as you keep giving it something else to do — school, work, relationships, survival, busyness. The problem is that it was never designed to do this indefinitely.

At some point the scaffolding thins. For some people that's age. For others it's a life transition — a breakup, a job loss, early sobriety, an empty house. The external regulators fall away and the material that's been waiting underneath finally has the space it needs to surface. Not because something new happened. Because nothing is in the way anymore.

At the same time, research published in The American Journal of Geriatric Psychiatry documents something clinicians have observed for decades but that rarely makes it into the cultural conversation around trauma: survivors of childhood abuse — physical, emotional, sexual — continue to meet full PTSD diagnostic criteria, or what researchers call subthreshold PTSD, well into their 70s and 80s. Not as echoes. Not as mild residue. As active, ongoing symptom profiles in elderly people who have carried this for fifty, sixty years.

And there is a specific trigger that makes it worse.

Retirement.

Clinical documentation identifies the transition out of active career and parenting as a point where trauma symptoms frequently intensify rather than ease. The reason is straightforward and brutal: for many survivors, work and active parenting have been functioning as lifelong regulation strategies — not consciously, not by design, but effectively. The structure, the purpose, the external demand on attention — all of it keeps the nervous system oriented outward. When that scaffolding comes down, the brain finally has the space it's been avoiding. The unresolved material that has been waiting underneath the busyness of a whole life surfaces. Not because something new happened. Because nothing is in the way anymore.

The distraction strategy has an expiry date. For a lot of people, it's called retirement.

The Research
The Body Has Been Keeping Score

While the brain has been managing, the body has been accumulating.

The Dunedin Study — a longitudinal study that has tracked a cohort of individuals from birth into their late 40s and 50s, and one you can read more about on this site — found that individuals who experienced childhood maltreatment carried significantly elevated levels of C-reactive protein decades later. CRP is a primary marker of systemic inflammation. It is also a precursor to the diseases that dominate elderly mortality: cardiovascular disease, diabetes, accelerated cognitive decline. The trauma isn't just psychological history. It's written into the immune system, still running a threat response for an environment that no longer exists.

But the Dunedin finding on inflammation is only part of the picture. The researchers also tracked not just where people ended up biologically, but how fast they were getting there — and the data on pace is as important as the data on outcomes. Trauma survivors weren't just biologically older at any given point in time. They were aging faster, on a measurably steeper trajectory. For many, old age will arrive earlier than it should. Not as a metaphor for how hard life has been. As a biological fact.

The more precise molecular evidence comes from epigenetic research. A 2023 study from McMaster University, drawing on data from the Canadian Longitudinal Study on Aging, found that high adverse childhood experience scores are directly linked to accelerated biological aging — with DNA methylation patterns, what researchers call epigenetic clocks, running measurably ahead of chronological age in trauma survivors. Your birth certificate says one thing. Your cells, rewritten by early life stress and still running the original programming, say another.

// A Practical Starting Point

If you want a concrete starting point, high-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) tests are available as at-home kits through most major pharmacies and online health platforms. They're inexpensive and require only a finger-prick blood sample. If your result comes back elevated — and for chronic stress and trauma histories, it frequently does — that's enough to warrant a conversation with your doctor. You don't need to walk in with a diagnosis. You walk in with a number and a history, and you let that open the door.

Takeaway...
Trauma leaves biological marks — in inflammation, in immune response, in accelerated aging. These aren't metaphors. They're measurable, and they compound over time.
// What You're Actually Waiting For

Put it together and here is what the evidence describes:

A nervous system that has been in low-grade threat response for decades, running inflammation as a baseline condition, aging faster than the calendar reflects, held in functional shape by the structure and demands of a working life — and then retirement comes, or the kids leave, or the friends start dying, and the scaffolding comes down.

The world gets smaller. That is not a poetic observation — it is what literally happens. The social network shrinks. Mobility decreases. The days get quieter. And for the person who has spent forty years using noise and motion and purpose as regulation, quiet is not peace. Quiet is the thing they've been outrunning.

And the cruelest part may be this: the attachment patterns that trauma instilled don't retire either. The push-pull, the walls, the reflexive withdrawal from people who get too close — these are not habits that soften with age. They are deeply encoded relational strategies the nervous system runs automatically, and late life is exactly the kind of threat-saturated environment that activates them hardest. Loss of autonomy. Physical vulnerability. Dependence on caregivers. For a survivor with unresolved attachment wounds, that environment doesn't trigger connection-seeking. It triggers the original defensive posture — withdrawal, suspicion, pushing away the people closest to them.

Research on attachment in aging populations, including work building on Bowlby's lifespan attachment theory and extended by Mikulincer & Shaver, documents this precisely: avoidant attachment predicts reduced help-seeking and social withdrawal in late life — when the need for support is at its highest. Clinicians working in dementia care have a term for what they observe in patients with trauma histories: hostile dependency — the person needs care and simultaneously rejects it, the nervous system running its original threat response on the people trying to help.

The social world shrinks partly because people die and leave. But for many survivors it shrinks faster — because the nervous system, still running its original defensive programming, does what it always did. It pushes people away. Not out of cruelty. Not out of choice. Out of a pattern that was never interrupted.

And the brain, meanwhile, is less equipped to manage any of it than it has ever been. The prefrontal override that kept the amygdala in check is weaker. The cognitive flexibility that allowed for reframing and context is reduced. What remains — with the kind of staying power that the Alive Inside footage makes impossible to look away from — is the emotional and autobiographical core. The stuff that was encoded early, encoded deep, encoded in the body.

Trauma memories are stored in the same emotionally-saturated, somatically-encoded systems that Henry's music reached. That is not a coincidence. It is the mechanism. And it means that for the person who never addressed their trauma, cognitive decline in old age does not erase the wound.

It erases the defenses.

The Case for Now

This page is not an argument for despair.

The research that documents what happens when trauma goes untreated is the same research that confirms trauma can be treated — that the nervous system retains plasticity, that the epigenetic damage is not wholly irreversible, that people well into adulthood make genuine and lasting recoveries.

But that work does not happen passively. It does not happen by accumulating sober years and assuming the distance is doing something. It requires intentional, targeted engagement with the material that the brain has been managing around for decades.

You are not just recovering for today. You are recovering for the person you will be at 75 — with less energy, fewer people, more silence, and less capacity to outrun what you didn't address. That person deserves the same intervention you're capable of seeking right now.

None of this is meant to suggest that everyone carries unresolved trauma, or that ordinary life stress leaves the same kind of landmine. But if trauma is part of your history — if there are memories you deliberately keep shoved down because bringing them forward is unbearable, or if you find yourself activated into fight or flight for reasons you can't explain — it's worth having a conversation with a professional. Not to excavate everything. Not to relive it. Just to make sure you're not building the next thirty years on top of something that's still live.

Time doesn't heal wounds.

It just gives them more room to grow roots.
